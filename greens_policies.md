# The Australian Greens — Signature Policies

**Purpose:** Party-platform source material for the Australian political compass survey. Drawn from the Greens' official site (greens.org.au — both the standing National policy platform and the 2025 election campaign material) and current reporting, compiled 19 June 2026.

> Faithful summary of what the party itself states — descriptive, not endorsing. Many headline dollar figures (50,000 refugee intake, 50c fares, $338bn corporate tax, $195bn dental) come from the **2025 election campaign**, not the standing platform — cite as "their 2025 platform," as these can change between elections.

**Other parties' positions:** under each policy, a *"Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand"* block records each party's stance relative to the Greens'. Tags: **Agree / Partly agree / Mixed / Oppose / No stated policy / Unsettled.** Positions are stated only where reliably known (from the verified bills in `labor_bills_party_positions.md`, established platforms, or current reporting) or reliably inferable; otherwise this is said plainly rather than guessed.

**Leaders & context as of mid-2026 (post–May 2025 election):** Greens led by **Larissa Waters** (since May 2025, after Adam Bandt lost his seat). At the 2025 election the Greens **fell to ~1 House seat** but **retained 11 Senate seats and now hold the sole balance of power in the Senate**, giving them strong leverage over Labor's legislation. Waters has signalled a **"firm but constructive"** posture (e.g. negotiating Labor's EPBC environment-law reforms in Nov 2025) while still campaigning hard against Labor on housing, tax, fossil fuels and Gaza. Other leaders: Labor — Albanese (re-elected); Coalition — Angus Taylor (Liberal) + Matt Canavan (Nationals).

## The 10 signature policies

### 1. Tax billionaires & big corporations
The revenue engine for everything else: a **10% annual wealth tax on Australia's ~150 billionaires**, a **40% corporate "super-profits"/tycoon tax** on companies turning over >$100m, and a **40% coal & mining super-profits tax** — plus restoring a broad resource-rent tax. Costed by the Greens (via the PBO) at hundreds of billions over a decade.
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/economic-justice, /platform/fair-share; tycoon-tax & billionaire-tax media releases.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose.** Rejects wealth and super-profits taxes; only modest multinational-tax measures. (It did add a tax on super balances over $3m — a sliver of overlap on taxing wealth, but nowhere near the Greens' scale.)
- **Coalition — Oppose (strongly).** Lower-tax party; against new wealth or super-profits taxes.
- **One Nation — Partly agree (resources/multinationals only).** Says multinationals should "pay their fair share" and wants gas royalties at the point of production (+$13bn) — but opposes broad wealth taxes and is otherwise low-tax.

### 2. End coal & gas — 75% emissions cut by 2030
**No new coal or gas projects**, phase out existing coal generation, **75% emissions cut on 2005 levels by 2030** and net zero by 2035, 100% renewables, and a levy to phase out coal exports.
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/climate-change-and-energy; coal-export-levy reporting.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose (but shares the climate-action direction).** Backs net zero 2050 + 43% by 2030, but supports continued gas (Future Gas Strategy) and keeps approving coal/gas — rejects the 75%/no-new-fossil agenda. Closest major party to the Greens here, by far.
- **Coalition — Oppose (opposite pole).** Pro-coal/gas and **dumped net zero in Nov 2025**.
- **One Nation — Oppose (opposite pole).** Pro-coal/gas/nuclear, reject the targets, exit Paris.

### 3. Dental & mental health into Medicare (free GP)
Add **dental and unlimited mental-health care to Medicare** and make GP visits free (tripled bulk-billing incentives, 1,000 free clinics) — treating healthcare as universal and fully public.
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/health, /policies/mental-health; dental-Medicare release.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Partly agree.** Strengthened bulk-billing, urgent-care clinics and cheaper medicines, but has **not** committed to universal dental-into-Medicare on cost grounds. Same direction, smaller scope.
- **Coalition — Mixed/Oppose.** Backs Medicare rhetorically but opposes large expansions and leans toward private health; unlikely to fund dental into Medicare.
- **One Nation — Partly agree (narrow).** Wants higher GP rebates / restored bulk-billing and a Medicare-fraud crackdown — overlaps on GP/bulk-billing, but not the universal dental/mental-health model.

### 4. Rent freeze & public housing + end investor tax breaks
A **two-year national rent freeze then a 2%-per-2-years cap**, a **public property developer** building affordable homes, and **phasing out negative gearing and the CGT discount** (beyond one investment property).
*Sources: greens.org.au/campaigns/rent-freeze-now, /policies/housing-and-homelessness.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Mixed (shifted in 2026).** Still resists rent freezes and a public developer, but its **2026 Budget reversed course and wound back negative gearing (new builds only) and the CGT discount from 1 July 2027** — substantially adopting a long-standing Greens demand. It had previously ruled this out, which drove the bitter 2023–24 housing standoffs in `labor_bills_party_positions.md`.
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Against rent controls and against scrapping investor tax breaks (defends negative gearing/CGT discount); opposes a government developer.
- **One Nation — Oppose (from the right).** Its housing fix is cutting migration + GST off building materials; opposes rent controls and removing investor tax breaks. (It does separately want to curb *foreign* property ownership.)

### 5. Wipe all student debt + free uni & TAFE
**Cancel all student (HELP/HECS) debt** and make **public university and TAFE free** (government covers all fees).
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/education; wipe-student-debt release.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Partly agree (much smaller).** Cut student debt 20% (2025), capped indexation and funded fee-free TAFE places — same direction, but rejects a full wipe or free university.
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Opposed the 20% cut in substance ("reverse Robin Hood"); against free uni / debt cancellation.
- **One Nation — Oppose (with one narrow overlap).** Hanson called debt write-offs "ridiculous," but One Nation proposes HECS forgiveness for doctors/nurses who serve in the regions — opposes the universal wipe.

### 6. End offshore detention + raise refugee intake
**Abolish offshore processing and indefinite/mandatory detention** ("Close the Camps"), **raise the humanitarian intake to 50,000**, grant permanent visas, and treat seeking asylum as a lawful right.
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/immigration-and-refugees, /campaigns/close-the-camps.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose.** Maintains offshore processing and boat turnbacks and toughened detention/removal (the NZYQ-era bills — see bills doc); won't lift the intake to 50,000.
- **Coalition — Oppose (opposite pole).** Hardline borders/detention; pushed Labor to go *harder*.
- **One Nation — Oppose (sharpest opposite).** Wants deep migration cuts, ~75,000 deportations and to exit the UN Refugee Convention.

### 7. End AUKUS, sanction Israel & recognise Palestine, nuclear disarmament
**Oppose AUKUS** and the nuclear-submarine path, **sanction Israel** and impose a two-way arms ban (alongside recognising Palestine), abolish nuclear weapons (ratify the TPNW), and redirect military spending to diplomacy/aid.
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/peace-conflict-response-and-veterans, /policies/nuclear-and-uranium; Palestine media releases.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose, with partial overlap on Palestine.** Strongly backs AUKUS; moved to **recognise Palestinian statehood** (2025) but rejects sanctions/an arms embargo on Israel. So: oppose on AUKUS, partial on Palestine recognition only.
- **Coalition — Oppose (opposite pole).** Strong AUKUS supporter and pro-Israel; opposes sanctions and Palestinian recognition.
- **One Nation — Mostly oppose / Unsettled.** No published defence policy (AUKUS stance unclear); generally pro-Israel and sovereigntist (wants to cut foreign aid, exit the UN) — not aligned with the Greens, though for different reasons. *Flag AUKUS as unconfirmed for One Nation.*

### 8. End native forest logging & protect nature
**End native-forest logging immediately**, protect old-growth forests and biodiversity, oppose fracking and large-scale land clearing, and strengthen federal environment law.
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/natural-resources-forests-and-mining, /policies/biodiversity.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Mixed.** Federally hasn't banned native-forest logging, but passed the Nature Repair market and negotiated **EPBC environment-law reforms with the Greens** (Nov 2025) excluding coal/gas from fast-tracking. Partial on nature protection; opposes a logging ban.
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Pro-forestry industry; against logging bans and tighter environmental restrictions.
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Pro-forestry (expand plantations, cut GST on timber, protect timber jobs); opposes restrictions.

### 9. Free universal childcare & cheaper essentials
A **free, universal public childcare system** (replacing the subsidy), plus cost-of-living relief through public services — **50-cent public transport fares**, free school lunches, cheaper essentials.
*Sources: greens.org.au/platform, /facts.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Partly agree.** Delivered Cheaper Child Care (90% subsidy) and is moving toward a "universal" childcare system / three-day guarantee — same direction, but not free/fully public.
- **Coalition — Mixed/Oppose.** Backed the 2022 cheaper-childcare changes but opposed the 2025 activity-test/three-day-guarantee expansion; against a free universal model.
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Prefers **family tax / income splitting** so a parent can stay home rather than use subsidised childcare.

### 10. Progressive social reform (drugs, LGBTQ+, First Nations)
**Decriminalise personal drug use and legalise/regulate cannabis** (pill testing, harm reduction); **ban conversion practices, fund gender-affirming care and affirm trans rights**; pursue **Treaty and a federal Truth and Justice Commission** with First Nations. *(Bundled here — distinct issues sharing a socially-progressive direction.)*
*Sources: greens.org.au/policies/reducing-alcohol-tobacco-and-other-drug-harm, /sexual-orientation-gender-identity-and-intersex, /first-nations-peoples-truth-treaty-voice.*

**Where Labor, Coalition & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Mixed.** Supports LGBTQ+ rights, but is cautious on drug decriminalisation (treats it as a state matter) and **shelved federal Treaty/Truth after the 2023 Voice referendum loss**. Partial on LGBTQ; cautious-to-oppose on the rest.
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Conservative on drugs, more conservative on trans issues, and opposed the Voice/Treaty.
- **One Nation — Oppose (opposite pole).** Strongly opposes "gender ideology"/trans rights and Treaty; anti-drug-liberalisation (though it does support *medicinal* cannabis — a narrow overlap).

## Notes for compass design
- The Greens anchor the **left-progressive** corner — the mirror image of One Nation. The sharpest four-party splits are on **refugees, climate ambition, tax, and culture**, which makes these the highest-information compass questions.
- **Labor sits between Greens and Coalition** on most economic/climate items — often "same direction, smaller scope" vs the Greens (health, education, childcare, nature) but a genuine **opponent** on housing tax breaks, rent control, refugees and AUKUS. This Labor-vs-Greens *intra-left* tension (supporting from the left) is valuable for separating "left" from "far-left/progressive."
- **One Nation is the Greens' clean opposite** on nearly everything except faint overlaps (taxing multinationals/resources; medicinal cannabis) — useful as an axis anchor, not a discriminating question.
- Watch the time-sensitive items: Labor's Palestine recognition (2025) and the Greens–Labor EPBC deal (Nov 2025) show the two left parties partly converging in the 48th Parliament — reflect the current state, not the 2023–24 standoff dynamic.
