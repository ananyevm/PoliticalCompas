# The Australian Labor Party — Signature Policies

**Purpose:** Party-platform source material for the Australian political compass survey. Compiled 19 June 2026 from Labor's first-term legislative record (see `labor_bills_party_positions.md`), its 2025 re-election platform, and its second-term agenda. Labor is the **governing party** (PM Anthony Albanese, Treasurer Jim Chalmers), re-elected 3 May 2025 — so its positions are the best-documented of the four.

**Other parties' positions:** under each policy, a *"Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand"* block records each party's stance relative to Labor's. Tags: **Agree / Partly agree / Mixed / Oppose / No stated policy / Unsettled.** Positions are stated only where reliably known or reliably inferable; otherwise this is said plainly rather than guessed.

**Note on Labor's positioning:** Labor governs from the **centre-left**, so on most planks the Greens sit to its left ("same direction, more ambitious") while the Coalition and One Nation oppose from the right — but Labor deliberately holds **centrist/right-leaning positions on borders and AUKUS**, which is where it overlaps with the Coalition. (Its **2026-Budget reversal on negative gearing/CGT** — see Policies 4 and 5 — moved housing tax sharply *toward* the Greens, removing what used to be a Labor–Coalition overlap.)

## The 10 signature policies

### 1. Strengthen Medicare — bulk-billing & cheaper medicines
Labor's flagship brand: an **$8.5bn Medicare boost** to reach **90% bulk-billing by 2030** (universal tripled bulk-billing incentive from Nov 2025) and **$25 PBS scripts** (from Jan 2026).
*Sources: pm.gov.au; The Conversation (PBS $25).*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Partly agree.** Matched Labor's bulk-billing investment at the 2025 election; supports Medicare but leans more toward private health.
- **Greens — Partly agree (want more).** Support it and would go further — dental and mental health into Medicare, free GP visits.
- **One Nation — Partly agree (narrower).** Wants higher GP rebates / restored bulk-billing and a Medicare-fraud crackdown.

### 2. Moderate climate action (net zero, but pro-gas)
Net zero by 2050, **≥43% by 2030 and a 62–70% by 2035 target**, the Safeguard Mechanism on big emitters, and staying in Paris — *while* backing continued **gas** ("to 2050 and beyond" under the Future Gas Strategy). A deliberately centrist climate position.
*Sources: pm.gov.au (2035 target); The Conversation (gas strategy).*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Oppose (post-2025).** Dumped net zero (Nov 2025), would abolish the Safeguard Mechanism and scrap the targets. (Overlaps only on supporting gas.)
- **Greens — Mixed.** Share the direction but say Labor is far too weak, and **strongly oppose Labor's gas support** and new fossil approvals — opposing from the left.
- **One Nation — Oppose (opposite pole).** Reject net zero and want to exit Paris.

### 3. Pro-worker industrial relations
Strengthen workers' rights: **multi-employer bargaining, "same job, same pay," casual-conversion rights, the right to disconnect, and criminalising wage theft** (the Secure Jobs/Closing Loopholes laws).
*Source: `labor_bills_party_positions.md`.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Voted against every bill and wants to roll the laws back.
- **Greens — Agree (pushed further).** Voted for all of them and *authored* the right-to-disconnect amendment.
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Voted against Labor's IR bills (anti-union/small-business framing).

### 4. Housing — build supply + help buyers + wind back investor tax breaks
A mixed approach: the **$10bn Housing Australia Future Fund**, **Help to Buy** shared equity, an expanded **5% deposit scheme**, and **$10bn for 100,000 homes** for first-home buyers. **In a major 2026-Budget reversal, Labor is now winding back investor tax breaks** — from 1 July 2027 negative gearing is limited to **new builds only**, and the 50% CGT discount is replaced (see Policy 5). This abandons Labor's prior promise to leave these untouched.
*Sources: pm.gov.au (tax reform, May 2026); loanmarket.com.au; The Conversation; bills doc.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Would abolish the HAFF, opposes Help to Buy (government co-ownership), and **opposes the 2026 negative-gearing/CGT changes** — it wants to keep the investor tax breaks (and prefers super-for-housing).
- **Greens — Mixed (a major 2026 win).** Still oppose Labor's overall model from the left (no rent freeze, no public developer), but Labor's wind-back of negative gearing/the CGT discount **substantially adopts a long-standing Greens demand** (they want it to go further — all properties, no grandfathering).
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Opposed the HAFF ("stock-market gamble") and Help to Buy ("dog of a bill"); prefers cutting migration + GST off building materials.

### 5. Tax: cost-of-living cuts, super reform & winding back property tax breaks
**Income-tax relief** (the Stage 3 redesign skewed to low/middle earners, plus "top-up" cuts in 2026 and 2027) and **energy-bill rebates**, paired with **Division 296** (an extra tax on super balances over $3m, revised in 2025 to realised gains only, indexed). The **2026–27 Budget (budget night 12 May 2026)** added a major property-tax reform: from **1 July 2027**, negative gearing is **limited to new builds** (established-property investors can no longer offset rental losses against wages — only against property income; existing owners grandfathered), and the **50% CGT discount is replaced with cost-base indexation plus a 30% minimum tax on realised gains** (new builds and pre-2027 accrued gains exempt; pensioners exempt from the minimum rate).
*Sources: pm.gov.au (tax reform for workers, businesses and future generations, May 2026); Baker McKenzie / William Buck budget analyses; Div 296 reporting.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Oppose (sharper now).** Backed the Stage 3 redesign but **opposes the $3m super tax and the 2026 negative-gearing/CGT changes**, vowing to keep the investor tax breaks; prefers indexing tax brackets instead.
- **Greens — Partly agree (want more).** Supported the tax cuts (wanting them more progressive), back taxing high super balances, and have **long campaigned for exactly this negative-gearing/CGT wind-back** — a significant win — though they want it to go further (all assets, no grandfathering) plus a billionaire/corporate super-profits tax.
- **One Nation — Mixed/Oppose.** Pro income-tax cuts (agree), but likely opposes the super tax and the new property-tax measures (anti-new-taxes; courts self-funded retirees) — *stance inferred, not a recorded vote.*

### 6. Cheaper education & childcare
**20% cut to all student (HELP) debt** (~$16bn wiped) plus a higher repayment threshold, **fee-free TAFE**, and a childcare **"Three-Day Guarantee"** (from Jan 2026) abolishing the activity test as a step toward universal childcare.
*Sources: The Conversation (HECS); education.gov.au (3-Day Guarantee); bills doc.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Mixed/Oppose.** Opposed the 20% HECS cut ("reverse Robin Hood") and the childcare expansion; supported the 2022 cheaper-childcare changes.
- **Greens — Partly agree (want more).** Backed the HECS cut as a step toward a full debt wipe and free uni/TAFE; want free universal childcare.
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Hanson called debt write-offs "ridiculous"; prefers family-tax/income-splitting over subsidised childcare.

### 7. Future Made in Australia
A **~$22.7bn green-industry policy** — production tax credits for renewable hydrogen and critical-minerals processing, plus green metals and clean manufacturing (Australia's response to the US IRA).
*Sources: treasury.gov.au; bills doc.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Oppose.** Called it "corporate welfare"/poor-value subsidy; voted against the production tax credits.
- **Greens — Partly agree.** Supported it (winning an amendment excluding uranium); back green industry while critiquing the detail.
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Opposes green-industry subsidies.

### 8. Managed migration & strong borders
A two-sided stance: **reduce net overseas migration toward pre-pandemic levels** (managed, ~225–235k, *not* slashed) while maintaining **Operation Sovereign Borders** — offshore processing, boat turnbacks and toughened detention/removal of non-citizens (the NZYQ-era bills).
*Sources: The Mandarin (migration); bills doc.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Partly agree (pushes harder).** Shares strong borders/offshore processing, but wants **deeper** migration cuts (a housing-linked cap) than Labor.
- **Greens — Oppose.** Oppose offshore detention, turnbacks and deportations; want a higher humanitarian intake.
- **One Nation — Partly agree (wants far harder).** Backs strong borders but wants much deeper cuts (130k) and mass deportations.

### 9. Integrity & reconciliation
The **National Anti-Corruption Commission**, **electoral donation/spending reform** (the 2025 deal), and the (defeated) **Voice to Parliament** referendum.
*Source: `labor_bills_party_positions.md`.*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Mixed.** Backed the NACC (bipartisan) and **did the electoral-reform deal with Labor**, but campaigned **No** on the Voice.
- **Greens — Mixed.** Backed the NACC (wanted it stronger) and campaigned **Yes** on the Voice, but opposed the electoral-reform "stitch-up."
- **One Nation — Oppose.** Opposed the NACC, campaigned No on the Voice, and opposed the electoral reform.

### 10. AUKUS & a values-based foreign policy
Full commitment to **AUKUS** (nuclear submarines; bipartisan) alongside **recognising the State of Palestine** (Sept 2025, with the UK and Canada) — a "principled middle" foreign policy.
*Sources: armyrecognition.com (AUKUS review); pm.gov.au (Palestine).*

**Where the Coalition, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Coalition — Mixed.** Strongly backs AUKUS (agree) but would **revoke** Palestinian recognition (oppose).
- **Greens — Mixed.** Oppose AUKUS, but support recognising Palestine and want to go further (sanctions + arms ban).
- **One Nation — Mixed/Unsettled.** No published defence policy (AUKUS stance unconfirmed); generally pro-Israel, so likely opposes Palestinian recognition.

## Notes for compass design
- **Labor is the centre/pivot of the four-party spread.** On most economic and climate planks it is "less than the Greens, more than the Coalition," which makes Labor-vs-Greens questions good for separating **centre-left from progressive-left**, and Labor-vs-Coalition questions good for the **mainstream left–right** divide.
- **Labor's right-leaning planks are the most useful cross-cutting questions:** strong borders/AUKUS (agrees with the Coalition, opposes the Greens). These map a **libertarian–authoritarian / globalist–sovereigntist** second axis rather than simple left–right. (Note: negative gearing **used** to be a Labor–Coalition overlap, but the 2026 Budget flipped Labor to the Greens' side — it's now a left–right *economic* question, not a cross-cutting one.)
- **Watch the 2025–26 convergences** already noted across the docs: Labor recognised Palestine and did the EPBC deal with the Greens, while the Coalition moved toward One Nation on climate/migration — so the four-party map in mid-2026 is *Greens ↔ Labor* (left, with tension) vs *Coalition ↔ One Nation* (right, with tension), rather than four evenly-spaced points.
