# The Coalition (Liberal–National) — Signature Policies

**Purpose:** Party-platform source material for the Australian political compass survey. Compiled 19 June 2026 from Liberal/National party material, Angus Taylor's May 2026 budget reply ("Plan to Restore Living Standards"), and current reporting.

> ⚠️ **Read this first — the Coalition's platform is partly in flux.** It lost the May 2025 election (under Peter Dutton, who also lost his seat), cycled through leaders (Dutton → Sussan Ley → **Angus Taylor**, Liberal leader since Feb 2026; **Matt Canavan** leads the Nationals since Mar 2026), and is **rebuilding policy in opposition** — several 2025 election commitments have been dropped, hardened, or had their numbers **deferred until closer to the next election**. Positions below reflect the *current* (mid-2026) Taylor/Canavan stance and flag what is **settled** vs **in flux**. This is the most uncertain of the four party docs.

**Other parties' positions:** under each policy, a *"Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand"* block records each party's stance relative to the Coalition's. Tags: **Agree / Partly agree / Mixed / Oppose / No stated policy / Unsettled.** Positions are stated only where reliably known or reliably inferable; otherwise this is said plainly rather than guessed.

## The 10 signature policies

### 1. Scrap net zero — coal, gas & nuclear energy
**Dump the net-zero-by-2050 and 43%-by-2030 targets** (done Nov 2025), abolish the Safeguard Mechanism ("a carbon tax") and the Net Zero Economy Authority, **back coal, gas and oil**, and **lift the ban on nuclear power**. *Settled:* dumping net zero/targets, pro-fossil, lift-the-ban. *In flux:* whether Dutton's specific 7-reactor / ~$331bn government build survives (Taylor affirms nuclear in principle); and **Paris** — Taylor/Canavan say *stay in* Paris, but the Nationals base is pushing to exit.
*Sources: liberal.org.au May 2026 plan; RenewEconomy; Bloomberg (net-zero dump).*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose (overlap on gas).** Keeps net zero 2050 + 43%, the Safeguard Mechanism, and the nuclear ban — but also backs gas (Future Gas Strategy). Opposite on targets/nuclear; aligned on gas.
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Want faster, deeper cuts, no new coal/gas, and are strongly anti-nuclear.
- **One Nation — Agree (closest).** Also rejects net zero, is pro-coal/gas/nuclear — and goes further by wanting to **exit Paris** (where Coalition leaders say stay).

### 2. "Tax Back Guarantee" — index income-tax brackets to inflation
Permanently **index income-tax thresholds to inflation** to end "bracket creep" (~$22.5bn over the forwards, phased from 2028–29), plus a **permanent $50,000 small-business instant asset write-off** and a "Future Generations Fund" banking resource windfalls for debt reduction.
*Settled:* indexation is the costed flagship. *In flux:* company-tax and deficit/savings detail.
*Sources: liberal.org.au; The Conversation (Taylor tax indexation).*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Mixed.** Delivered its own income-tax cuts (Stage 3 redesign) and a smaller instant write-off, but **opposes indexing brackets** (prefers discretionary cuts). Same goal (tax relief), different mechanism.
- **Greens — Oppose.** Favour taxing the wealthy; see bracket indexation as skewed to higher earners and as draining revenue for services.
- **One Nation — Partly agree.** Low-tax instinct (wants a higher tax-free threshold for retirees, cheaper fuel) — sympathetic direction, no identical policy.

### 3. Cut migration sharply (housing-linked cap)
"One of the biggest cuts to immigration in Australian history": **cap Net Overseas Migration at the number of homes built** the prior year, cut international-student numbers and raise visa fees, and a two-year ban on foreign buyers of existing homes.
*Settled:* the housing-linked-cap mechanism and direction. *In flux:* the **exact NOM number is deferred** to "closer to the election" (2025's 185k→140k permanent-migration figures are not currently re-committed).
*Sources: The Conversation (Grattan, budget reply); SBS.*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose.** Backs higher (though now falling) managed migration; rejects a hard cap.
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Want a higher humanitarian intake; reject caps.
- **One Nation — Agree (goes further).** Wants a 130k visa cap — same direction, even harder.

### 4. Hardline borders, deportation & a "values" test
Maintain Operation Sovereign Borders (offshore processing, boat turnbacks), plus a Taylor-era **ICE-style joint-agency taskforce to deport ~65,000 visa overstayers**, mandatory social-media disclosure/screening of visa applicants, a legislated "Australian values"/English test, and restricting welfare/NDIS to citizens.
*Settled:* borders + the deportation/values package are heavily promoted flagships. (Critics call it "Trumpian.")
*Sources: Canberra Times (ICE-style plan); SBS (migration crackdown).*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Partly agree.** Shares Operation Sovereign Borders and toughened detention/removal (the NZYQ-era bills in `labor_bills_party_positions.md`) — but not the ICE-style taskforce or values test.
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Want to end offshore detention and oppose deportations.
- **One Nation — Agree.** Hardline borders, mass deportation and values-based entry — aligned, even harder (deport ~75,000; exit the Refugee Convention).

### 5. Market-side housing: super for housing, mortgage deductibility, keep investor tax breaks
Let first-home buyers **withdraw up to 40% of super ($50k cap)**, make **mortgage interest tax-deductible** on new-build first homes, **abolish the Housing Australia Future Fund**, and **keep negative gearing and the CGT discount** — vowing to **repeal Labor's 2026-Budget wind-back of those investor tax breaks**. Plus a supply/infrastructure fund and cheaper building codes.
*In flux:* sources **conflict** on whether super-for-housing is still flagship (reaffirmed by Taylor in some reporting; absent from the May 2026 plan in other reporting) — treat as likely retained but de-emphasised.
*Sources: liberal.org.au; savings.com.au (super-for-housing); The Conversation.*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose (shifted in 2026).** Opposes super-for-housing and mortgage deductibility and defends the HAFF — and its **2026 Budget reversed its old position, winding back negative gearing (new builds only) and the CGT discount**, so it no longer agrees with the Coalition on keeping investor tax breaks.
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Against all demand-side/investor measures; want to scrap negative gearing/the CGT discount and build public housing with rent caps.
- **One Nation — Partly agree.** Also wants to abolish the HAFF (called it a "stock-market gamble"); no clear position on super-for-housing — its own housing fix is GST off building materials + cutting migration.

### 6. Roll back Labor's industrial-relations laws
Unwind Labor's workplace agenda — **abolish the "right to disconnect," roll back multi-employer bargaining, scrap the new casual-conversion rights, and review "same job, same pay."** (Jane Hume is deputy leader and shadow IR.)
*In flux:* most specifics are pre-2025-election commitments not yet re-issued as a 2026 document; "same job same pay" is framed only as a "review."
*Sources: Clayton Utz (2025 IR); Taylor shadow ministry.*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose.** These are Labor's own flagship laws (see the Fair Work/Closing Loopholes bills).
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Want stronger worker protections; the Greens *authored* the right-to-disconnect amendment.
- **One Nation — Partly agree.** Voted against Labor's IR bills — sympathetic to rolling them back (anti-union/small-business framing), though it has no published "repeal" policy.

### 7. AUKUS, 3% defence spending & a hawkish foreign policy
**Strongly back AUKUS** ("fully funded"), lift **defence spending to at least 3% of GDP** (Taylor rejected US pressure for 3.5%), take a tougher line on China, and **revoke Australia's recognition of a Palestinian state** (declared Aug 2025).
*Settled:* AUKUS + 3% target. *In flux:* the Palestine-revocation pledge predates Taylor's leadership and hasn't been re-confirmed verbatim.
*Sources: SBS (defence spend); The Conversation (Palestine revocation).*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Mixed.** Strongly backs AUKUS (agree), but defence spending is lower than 3% and it **recognised Palestinian statehood** in 2025 (opposite of revoking).
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Anti-AUKUS, want lower military spending, and pro-Palestine (sanctions + recognition).
- **One Nation — Partly agree / Unsettled.** Generally pro-Israel (would likely align against Palestinian recognition) and sovereigntist, but has **no published defence policy** — its AUKUS stance is unconfirmed.

### 8. Smaller government — cut "waste" & abolish Labor's agencies/funds
"End big government": cut spending, **abolish the climate department, the Net Zero Economy Authority and the Housing Australia Future Fund**, and restrict welfare/services to citizens.
*In flux:* Dutton's specific **41,000 public-service-job cut and the work-from-home crackdown were dropped** as election liabilities and not re-committed; the **ABC position is unstated** — don't impute one.
*Sources: Canberra Times / The Mandarin (APS cuts); The Conversation.*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose.** Defends these agencies, funds and the public service.
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Strongly pro public services and these institutions.
- **One Nation — Agree (goes further).** Wants ~$90bn in cuts, to abolish the climate department and Net Zero Authority, and to exit the UN/WHO.

### 9. Oppose the Voice & "identity politics"; religious freedom
Opposed (and campaigned against) the **Indigenous Voice to Parliament**, scepticism toward Welcome to Country ceremonies and DEI/"identity politics" (Trump-aligned framing), and a shadow commitment to a **Religious Freedom/Discrimination bill** protecting religious schools.
*Settled:* Voice opposition. *In flux:* the religious-freedom bill is an intent, not detailed; specific anti-DEI/Welcome-to-Country funding pledges weren't re-confirmed after a portfolio reshuffle; **no reliable current Coalition position on trans/gender issues** — not imputed.
*Sources: reporting on Welcome to Country; Religious-freedom-bill coverage.*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Oppose.** Championed the Voice; supports anti-discrimination protections (its own religious-discrimination bill was shelved — see bills doc).
- **Greens — Oppose (opposite pole).** Pro-Voice/Treaty, pro-LGBTQ+/trans rights, oppose religious exemptions.
- **One Nation — Agree.** Opposed the Voice and is anti-"gender ideology"/anti-"woke" — aligned in direction (though more radical, and frames it as constitutional free speech rather than religious freedom).

### 10. Cost-of-living relief via cheaper fuel, cheaper energy & competition
Cut the cost of living through **lower fuel costs** (a temporary fuel-excise halving to ~26c/L), **cheaper energy** via coal/gas/nuclear, fuel-security reserves, and **supermarket divestiture powers** to break up retailers that abuse market power.
*In flux:* the supermarket-divestiture and instant-write-off details weren't re-confirmed for 2026.
*Sources: liberal.org.au; Spectator (fuel excise).*

**Where Labor, Greens & One Nation stand:**
- **Labor — Mixed.** Delivered energy-bill rebates, tax cuts and cheaper medicines and ran an ACCC supermarket inquiry — but opposes the Coalition's fossil-energy approach and forced divestiture.
- **Greens — Mixed.** **Agree on cracking down on supermarkets** (the Greens also pushed divestiture/anti-price-gouging) but oppose fuel-excise cuts (climate) and the fossil-energy approach.
- **One Nation — Partly agree.** Strong overlap on cheaper fuel (halve excise) and cheaper energy via coal/gas/nuclear; supermarket divestiture isn't a stated One Nation priority.

## Notes for compass design
- The Coalition is the **mainstream centre-right** anchor, but under Taylor it has **shifted toward One Nation** on energy (net zero dumped) and immigration (housing-linked cap, deportation taskforce) — so on those two axes Coalition and One Nation now **largely agree**, which weakens their power to *distinguish* the two parties. Use the *degree* (130k cap & exit-Refugee-Convention vs a softer cap) and culture/sovereignty items (exit UN/WHO, exit Paris, constitutional free speech) to separate them.
- **Labor sits to the Coalition's left on most things but overlaps on the security/border axis** (AUKUS, Operation Sovereign Borders, detention/removal) and, notably, on **keeping negative gearing/CGT** — good "cross-cutting" questions for a libertarian–authoritarian second dimension.
- **The Greens are the clean opposite** on nearly every Coalition plank — strongest contrasts on climate, immigration, defence/AUKUS, housing tax breaks and culture.
- **Caveat for question-writing:** because so much of the Coalition platform is *deferred or unsettled* in mid-2026, prefer compass questions anchored on its **settled flagships** (scrap net zero, cut migration, index tax brackets, AUKUS/3%, oppose the Voice) rather than the in-flux items (exact migration numbers, super-for-housing, IR specifics, ABC, Paris).
